# Write Persuasive Narratives

## Objective

Design a discovery the reader completes. Do not treat persuasion as proving a thesis to a passive audience.

Model the reader as a person already moving along a cognitive trajectory shaped by experience, identity, desire, shame, fear, social position, and unresolved contradictions. Enter that trajectory, place truthful clues, and let the reader retain ownership of the conclusion.

Read [references/theory.md](references/theory.md) when you need the full model or must distinguish viewpoint change from behavior change. Read [references/examples.md](references/examples.md) when choosing a narrative pattern or evaluating a draft.

## Build the persuasion context

Before drafting, identify:

```yaml
audience:
  recognizable_situation: ""
  current_self_explanation: ""
  unspoken_feelings: []
  internal_conflicts: []
  dignity_boundaries: []
  desired_future: ""
  feared_future: ""

influence_target:
  type: "viewpoint_change | behavior_change"
  current_trajectory: ""
  intended_trajectory: ""
  minimum_action: ""
  action_friction: []

narrative_material:
  lived_scenes: []
  anomalies: []
  clues: []
  sideways_proofs: []
  historical_parallels: []
  inference_gap: ""
```

If these fields are unknown, infer cautiously from supplied material and label the inference. Do not invent a vulnerable audience profile merely to improve conversion.

## Draft through trajectory correction

1. **Enter through life.** Open with a concrete situation the reader can recognize, not an abstract thesis.
2. **Name the unspoken conflict.** Articulate what the reader feels but may not say publicly.
3. **Protect dignity without lying.** Remove false shame by changing the causal frame only when the new frame is supportable.
4. **Place an anomaly.** Introduce a truthful detail the old explanation cannot fully explain.
5. **Use evidence as clues.** Let each fact alter interpretation; do not stack citations as a wall around the conclusion.
6. **Delay the thesis.** Keep the author’s intended answer out of sight while the reader reconstructs the situation.
7. **Leave an inference gap.** Stop one step before the final conclusion when the reader has enough material to complete it.
8. **Add a path only after recognition.** Introduce the new possibility after the reader has identified the problem in their own terms.
9. **Bridge to action when required.** Give a small, reversible experiment with visible cost and risk. Do not demand belief, identity conversion, purchase, or commitment as the first action.
10. **End with retained agency.** The reader should feel able to decide, test, compare, or leave.

## Choose the correct endpoint

For **viewpoint change**, aim to alter explanation:

```text
old interpretation
→ unresolved contradiction
→ truthful clues
→ self-generated reframe
```

For **behavior change**, continue:

```text
self-generated reframe
→ desirable but credible future
→ visible path
→ sense of agency
→ reduced friction
→ specific trigger
→ minimum action
```

Do not assume a changed opinion automatically produces behavior.

## Control the body-state layer

Write for experienced states such as safety, recognition, tension, relief, warmth, anger, or possibility. Treat language as meaning processed through memory, identity, and context—not as a direct control surface for hormones, a brain region, men, or women.

Do not use neuroscience vocabulary to disguise unsupported claims.

## Use sideways proof

Prefer observable consequences over self-declaration.

- Do not say a restaurant is trusted; show the returning customer whose order is remembered.
- Do not say a consultant has experience; show peers sending difficult cases before making a decision.
- Do not say a workflow saves time; show the nightly task that disappears or survives an employee departure.

Let the reader infer the quality from its trace in reality.

## Remove AI-preaching patterns

Reject or rewrite drafts that:

- announce the thesis before establishing the reader’s world;
- use “first, second, third” only to prove the author is correct;
- repeatedly summarize conclusions the reader already understands;
- insert empathy phrases without specific lived detail;
- turn every paragraph into a lesson;
- expose the sales solution before the reader has formed the relevant question;
- use absolute claims, false urgency, or universal promises;
- end with a grand call to action when a small experiment is more credible.

## Ethical boundary

Do not:

- fabricate facts, experiences, consensus, scarcity, authority, or testimonials;
- exploit grief, illness, poverty, loneliness, shame, cognitive impairment, or dependency;
- create enemies to force identity alignment;
- produce threat without a truthful and effective exit;
- hide material risks or remove meaningful alternatives;
- simulate the reader’s inner life with unwarranted certainty;
- optimize for involuntary compliance.

Distinguish ethical influence from manipulation by checking truthfulness, transparency, reader choice, reversibility, proportionality, and foreseeable harm.

## Quality test

Before returning the draft, ask:

1. Can the reader see their life before seeing the thesis?
2. Does each detail change interpretation rather than merely decorate the prose?
3. Did the reader complete any important inference?
4. Is the new path credible, bounded, and testable?
5. Did the draft preserve dignity and meaningful choice?
6. Would the piece still work if explicit claims of quality, authority, and opportunity were removed?

If not, revise the narrative structure rather than adding more arguments.
